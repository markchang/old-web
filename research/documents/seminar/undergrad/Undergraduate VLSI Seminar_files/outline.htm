Why Physical Design?
For the CAD Developer:
Rapidly developing field with many classic algorithms
Fiduccia-Mattheyses, Simulated Annealing,

Why Physical Design?
For the VLSI Designer:
Most chip design is highly automated
Chip complexity in 10M-100M transistors on a chip
Intel McKinley is projected to have 221M transistors
Physical Design is the compiler for designs
Understanding how specification becomes circuits guides logic design
Understanding errors/problems/restrictions important for the designer
Basic CMOS
CMOS is multiple layers of conducting material, separated by insulation.
Contacts/vias are cuts in the insulation to connect layers
The interaction of Polysilicon & Diffusion creates transistors
Full Custom Design
Using Full-Custom Design we can get exactly what we want.
However:
1) Complex to design
2) Takes weeks to fabricate
3) No economies of scale
4) How do we automate the mapping?
Standard Cells
Develop predefined implementations of basic gates with standard form-factor
Standard Cells
Use regular layout
Can automate the mapping process, but
1) Takes weeks to fabricate
2) No economies of scale
Combine Custom & Standard Cells
Use full custom for regular structures & critical paths
Standard cells handle complex logic & noncritical logic

Circuit Terminology
We represent circuits as graphs:
Nodes/Cells/Vertices  logic elements
Nets/Edges  wires in the design
Terminals  connections between nodes and nets
Partitioning Terminology
Cutset - set of nets connected to nodes in different partitions
Cutsize - number of nets in the cutset
Gain - change in cutset if a given node is moved
Floorplanning
Assign portions of a design to regions of the chip area
Blocks have adjustable sizes
Seek to reduce routing delay & area

Define a floorplan via a tree (hierarchy)

Placement
Pick relative location for each gate
Seek to improve routeability, limit delay, reduce overall area
Placement Restrictions
Rectangular chip
I/Os at periphery
Placement Goals
Reduce Area
Reduce height
Fewer signals per routing channel
Reduce width
Fewer feedthroughs
Balanced row width
Reduce Delay
Reduce wirelength
Placement Cost: Wirelength
Most systems use Manhattan routing (North, South, East, West, no diagonals)
Wirelength estimate = 1/2*(perimeter of bounding box) = Semi-perimeter
Placement Cost: Cutsize
Routing area estimate = row width*(Schannel heights) + feedthroughs
Problem: dont know where a signal will be routed
Maximum vertical cutsize estimates channel height
Maximum horizontal cutsize estimates feedthroughs
Annealing
Annealing: Cooling hot metals to form good crystal structures
Start at high temperatures - atoms move randomly about
Cool at specific cooling schedule - leave enough time for atoms to attract into crystal lattice
Simulated Annealing
Move nodes randomly
Initially high temperature - allow bad moves to happen
Lower temperature, accepting less and less bad moves
Slowly cool placement to allow good structure to form
Global Routing
Determine overall path of all routes
Pick channels to route through
Seeks to reduce delay, channel widths
Detailed Routing
Determine exactly how each signal is routed through each region
Seeks to reduce routing area
Compaction
Squeeze layout to reduce chip area
Helps eliminate inefficiencies caused by other steps
